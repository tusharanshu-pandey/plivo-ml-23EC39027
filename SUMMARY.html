Result

For every pause in a user turn we output p_eot from causal prosodic features (audio strictly before the pause). Honest grouped-by-turn out-of-fold, overall AUC 0.685:

Metric = mean response delay at ≤5% interrupted turns (lower is better). English improves ~24%. Hindi sits at its acoustic ceiling — see below.

Approach

A soft-voting ensemble — a regularized LogisticRegression + a bagged shallow GradientBoosting (3 seeds) — over 30 causal features, trained on pooled English+Hindi. Pooling was validated: a Hindi-only model scored worse on Hindi (AUC 0.635 vs 0.706), i.e. English prosody transfers. We picked this over a single HistGradientBoosting that memorized the training set (in-sample AUC 1.0) yet lost out-of-fold.

What the features are (and where they came from)

Standardized LogisticRegression weights. ★ = feature added because of a human error-listening pass (see below), not generic prosody:

The two strongest signals are both human-driven: f0_final_pctl (final pitch at the bottom of the speaker's range ⇒ finished, even on a flat number) and voiced_seg_dur_std (steady digit-reading rhythm), plus energy fade-to-floor.

How we got here (RUNLOG in brief)

  1. v1 generic turn-final prosody + HistGB → AUC 0.66, but overfit (in-sample AUC 1.0).
  2. Listened to the 12 worst errors → the misses were English turn-ends on numbers/addresses that end flat (no pitch fall); "abruptness" appeared in both classes. Added pitch declination-to-floor, energy fade, list/number rhythm → linear AUC 0.62→0.66.
  3. Model bake-off → dropped the overfit booster for the LR + bagged-GBM ensemble (AUC 0.686).
  4. Listened to the worst Hindi errors (native speaker) → ends fade out ("shukriya", "karlunga"), dangerous holds cut abruptly mid-dictation ("panch… likh lijiye"); one "hold" was a silent click scored 0.88. Added fade-vs-cut + a silence guard → English 1232→1209 ms.
  5. Hindi ceiling confirmed: verb-final Hindi ends on a short auxiliary whose identity is lexical, plus genuine label noise (holds a native speaker hears as finished). Acoustics + the ≤5% budget cannot go below 850 ms here.

Human vs coding agent (honest split)

Coding agent (Claude Code): all code (features, training, predict.py, the error-dump & summary tools), every run, the model bake-off, and the coefficient analysis.

Human (candidate): listened to the worst error clips in both English and native Hindi and identified (a) the flat number/address endings, (b) fade-out vs abrupt-cut, and (c) the silent-click data artifact — the insights that produced the top-weighted features and turned generic-prosody v1 (AUC 0.66) into the final model. Also decided to trust the robust Hindi 850 ms over a lucky single-seed 808 ms, and to keep pooled training after validating it helps Hindi.

Why it beats the status quo

The silence-only endpointer waits a fixed timer (1600 ms English). By scoring end-of-turn from prosody it reaches the true end ~24% faster on English at the same 5% interruption budget, and it does so causally (features only from audio before the pause) so it is deployable in a live agent. The honest limits — Hindi's lexical/verb-final ceiling and dataset label noise — are documented rather than hidden, with a concrete next step (a causal frame-level sequence model + a from-scratch final-syllable cue) in NOTES.md.

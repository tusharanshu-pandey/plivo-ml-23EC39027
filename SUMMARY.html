End-of-Turn Detection

Tusharanshu Pandey · 23EC39027 · STT track · CPU-only, no pretrained models

Result

For every pause in a user turn we output p_eot from causal prosodic features (audio strictly before the pause). Honest grouped-by-turn out-of-fold, averaged over 5 random fold assignments (single-split numbers proved untrustworthy — see "Evaluation noise" below), overall AUC 0.654 ± 0.011:

EnglishHindi
Silence-only baseline1600 ms850 ms
This model1230 ± 32 ms834 ± 22 ms

Metric = mean response delay at ≤5% interrupted turns (lower is better). English improves ~23%; Hindi is statistically level with the (already strong) silence baseline — at the ceiling of the current features, not of the data (see below).

Approach

A soft-voting ensemble — a regularized LogisticRegression + a bagged shallow GradientBoosting (3 seeds) — over 30 causal features, trained on pooled English+Hindi. Pooling was validated: a Hindi-only model scored worse on Hindi (AUC 0.635 vs 0.706), i.e. English prosody transfers. We picked this over a single HistGradientBoosting that memorized the training set (in-sample AUC 1.0) yet lost out-of-fold.

The scorer sweeps threshold & delay itself, so we optimize separability, not a timer. That means down-ranking mid-turn holds and up-ranking true ends:

The two strongest signals are both human-driven: f0_final_pctl (final pitch at the bottom of the speaker's range ⇒ finished, even on a flat number) and voiced_seg_dur_std (steady digit-reading rhythm), plus energy fade-to-floor.

How we got here (RUNLOG in brief)

  1. v1 generic turn-final prosody + HistGB → AUC 0.66, but overfit (in-sample AUC 1.0).
  2. Listened to the 12 worst errors → the misses were English turn-ends on numbers/addresses that end flat (no pitch fall); "abruptness" appeared in both classes. Added pitch declination-to-floor, energy fade, list/number rhythm → linear AUC 0.62→0.66.
  3. Model bake-off → dropped the overfit booster for the LR + bagged-GBM ensemble (AUC 0.686).
  4. Listened to the worst Hindi errors (native speaker) → ends fade out ("shukriya", "karlunga"), dangerous holds cut abruptly mid-dictation ("panch… likh lijiye"); one "hold" was a silent click scored 0.88. Added fade-vs-cut + a silence guard (single-split English 1232→1209 ms — later shown to be within evaluation noise).
  5. Hindi limit diagnosed: verb-final Hindi ends on a short auxiliary whose identity is lexical, plus genuine label noise (holds a native speaker hears as finished). With the current features the ≤5% budget blocks anything below the baseline delay.
  6. Robustness guard: pauses with < 0.2 s of history got a zero feature vector that the model scored 0.63 (fires at any threshold). 0/496 dev pauses hit this, but the hidden set might — predict.py now emits a safe 0.02 for them.
  7. Evaluation noise measured: repeating the grouped CV over 5 random fold assignments showed ±30–45 ms delay noise — larger than most logged deltas. Two plausible "wins" (duration-weighted hold samples; causal pause-history features) failed replication and were rejected. All headline numbers are now mean ± std across seeds.

Evaluation noise & where the metric actually lives

Single-split numbers lie at this sample size. The previously reported EN 1209 / HI 850 came from one fortunate fold assignment; across 5 assignments the honest estimate is EN 1230 ± 32 ms, HI 834 ± 22 ms.

Hindi's operating point is the silence baseline's policy: the scorer picks threshold 0.05 (every pause fires) with a 0.85 s delay set purely by the handful of holds longer than 0.85 s — despite Hindi AUC ≈ 0.71, the ranking isn't yet usable there. The path to beating it is separating true ends from only the long (>0.5 s) holds: 83% of Hindi holds are shorter than 0.5 s and can never cause a false cutoff at that delay. On English the mean is dominated by the 54% of true ends that miss the threshold and pay the full 1.6 s timeout.

Human vs coding agent (honest split)

Coding agent (Claude Code): all code (features, training, predict.py, the error-dump & summary tools), every run, the model bake-off, and the coefficient analysis.

Human (candidate): listened to the worst error clips in both English and native Hindi and identified (a) the flat number/address endings, (b) fade-out vs abrupt-cut, and (c) the silent-click data artifact — the insights that produced the top-weighted features and turned generic-prosody v1 (AUC 0.66) into the final model. Also decided to trust robust numbers over lucky ones (Hindi 850 vs a single-seed 808), and to keep pooled training after validating it helps Hindi.

Second pass (agent-driven audit, human-approved): the headroom analysis of the scorer, the zero-vector robustness bug and its fix, the repeated-CV noise measurement that corrected the headline numbers, and the two rejected-as-noise experiments (duration-weighted holds, pause-history features).

Why it beats the status quo

The silence-only endpointer waits a fixed timer (1600 ms English). By scoring end-of-turn from prosody it reaches the true end ~23% faster on English at the same 5% interruption budget, and it does so causally (features only from audio before the pause) so it is deployable in a live agent. The honest limits — Hindi's lexical/verb-final dependence, dataset label noise, and the measured ±30 ms evaluation noise — are documented rather than hidden, with a concrete next step (attack the long-hold-vs-eot separation the metric actually depends on; a causal frame-level sequence model) in NOTES.md.
